# XRF Sediment Geochemistry of the Pebas Formation: Research Hypotheses and Literature Framework

## Draft Research Skeleton for Publication

**Working Title:** High-resolution XRF geochemistry reveals cyclic paleoenvironmental variability in the Miocene Pebas Mega-Wetland of Western Amazonia

---

## 1. Introduction and Rationale

### 1.1 The Pebas Mega-Wetland System

The Pebas Formation (= Solimoes Formation in Brazil) represents one of the largest freshwater systems in Earth history, covering >1 million km² across Colombian, Peruvian, Ecuadorian, and Brazilian Amazonia during the Miocene (c. 23-8 Ma). This long-lived lake-wetland system was shaped by:

- **Andean uplift** creating accommodation space and blocking Pacific drainage (Hoorn et al., 2010)
- **Dynamic topography** controlling basin subsidence (Eakin et al., 2014)
- **Orbital forcing** regulating transgressive-regressive cycles (Bogota-Angel et al., 2021)
- **Episodic marine connections** to the Caribbean (Wesselingh et al., 2002; Jaramillo et al., 2017)

**Key Knowledge Gap:** Despite extensive biostratigraphic and isotopic work, high-resolution sediment geochemistry using XRF core scanning has not been systematically applied to Pebas Formation outcrops. This limits understanding of short-term paleoenvironmental variability and the geochemical expression of orbital cycles.

### 1.2 Study Rationale

XRF core scanning provides continuous, high-resolution (sub-millimeter) geochemical records ideal for:

1. Detecting cyclic patterns at sub-Milankovitch to precession scales
2. Characterizing rapid paleoenvironmental transitions
3. Establishing geochemical facies for correlation
4. Validating isotope-based paleoenvironmental interpretations

## 2. Research Hypotheses

### Hypothesis 1: Redox Cycling Records Orbital-Scale Lake Level Fluctuations

**Statement:** Fe/Mn ratios in Pebas Formation sediments record cyclic redox variations driven by orbital-scale changes in lake depth and water column stratification.

**Predictions:**
- Fe/Mn should show systematic variations at obliquity (41 kyr) or precession (19-23 kyr) periodicities
- High Fe/Mn intervals (reducing conditions) should correlate with transgressive (deeper lake) phases
- Low Fe/Mn intervals should correlate with regressive phases and lignite development

**Literature Support:**
- Bogota-Angel et al. (2021) documented 41 kyr obliquity-driven depositional cycles at Los Chorros
- Wesselingh et al. (2002) described coarsening-upward parasequences capped by lignites
- Naher et al. (2013) validated Mn/Fe as bottom water oxygenation proxy in lakes

**Testable via:** Spectral analysis of Fe/Mn profiles; comparison with lithofacies cyclicity

---

### Hypothesis 2: Ca Variability Reflects Authigenic Carbonate vs. Terrigenous Dilution

**Statement:** Calcium concentration in Pebas sediments primarily records authigenic/biogenic carbonate production modulated by terrigenous input from Andean and cratonic sources.

**Predictions:**
- Ca should anticorrelate with Ti (terrigenous indicator)
- High Ca intervals should coincide with shell-rich horizons and ostracod-bearing layers
- Ca/Ti ratio should be higher during dry/evaporative phases (reduced runoff)

**Literature Support:**
- Vonhof et al. (1998) documented freshwater carbonate precipitation in Pebas lakes
- Wesselingh (2006) described abundant mollusc shell beds in the formation
- Carbonate-detrital mixing is fundamental to lacustrine geochemistry (Croudace & Rothwell, 2015)

**Complication:** Our redundancy analysis shows Ca/Ti ratio (r=0.89 with Ca) adds minimal information beyond raw Ca counts. Recommend interpreting Ca directly rather than normalized ratio.

---

### Hypothesis 3: Grain Size Proxies Record Hydrodynamic Energy Changes

**Statement:** Zr/Rb ratios in Pebas sediments systematically record changes in depositional energy, distinguishing high-energy deltaic/fluvial facies from low-energy lacustrine facies.

**Predictions:**
- High Zr/Rb (coarser sediment) should occur in channelized units with IHS (inclined heterolithic stratification)
- Low Zr/Rb (finer sediment) should characterize blue clay intervals and distal lacustrine facies
- Zr/Rb should show step-changes at facies boundaries

**Literature Support:**
- Gingele et al. (2007) validated Zr/Rb as grain size proxy in lacustrine sediments
- Hovius & Stark (2006) linked grain size to sediment transport energy
- Tamshiyacu locality shows tidally-influenced channelized deposits (Hovikoski et al., 2010)

**Site-Specific Prediction:** Santa Corina (SC) may show higher Zr/Rb variability if positioned closer to active channel systems compared to Tamshiyacu (TAM).

---

### Hypothesis 4: TAM and SC Record Different Depositional Sub-Environments

**Statement:** Geochemical signatures systematically differ between Tamshiyacu (TAM) and Santa Corina (SC) localities, reflecting their positions within the Pebas wetland mosaic.

**Predictions:**
| Parameter | TAM | SC | Interpretation |
|-----------|-----|-----|----------------|
| Mean Fe/Mn | Lower | Higher | SC more reducing (deeper/stratified) |
| Ca variability | Higher | Lower | TAM more carbonate-rich (shallower) |
| Zr/Rb range | Lower | Higher | SC more variable energy regime |
| Facies distribution | Carbonate-dominated | Siliciclastic-dominated | Sub-environment position |

**Literature Support:**
- Wesselingh et al. (2002) documented spatial heterogeneity in Pebas sub-environments
- Hoorn et al. (2022) emphasized the mosaic nature of the mega-wetland
- Ichnological data suggest variable marine influence across localities (Hovikoski et al., 2007)

---

### Hypothesis 5: Marine/Brackish Incursion Events Are Geochemically Distinct

**Statement:** Episodic oligohaline-mesohaline incursions documented by faunal and isotopic evidence should produce distinctive geochemical signatures in XRF data.

**Predictions:**
- Incursion intervals should show elevated Sr (marine/aragonite signal)
- Sr/Ca should increase during incursion events
- Possible enrichment in Ba, S (sulfate-bearing waters)
- Fe/Mn may decrease (more oxic marine waters)

**Literature Support:**
- Wesselingh et al. (2006) documented rare oligohaline-mesohaline intervals
- Vonhof et al. (2003) used 87Sr/86Sr to identify marine-influenced layers
- Jaramillo et al. (2017) identified two distinct Miocene marine flooding events

**Challenge:** True marine incursions are rare in Pebas Formation; may not be present in TAM/SC sections. Alternative: detect freshwater end-member variations (Andean vs. cratonic).

---

### Hypothesis 6: Provenance Signals Distinguish Andean vs. Cratonic Input

**Statement:** Geochemical proxies (particularly K/Ti and heavy mineral indicators) can distinguish sediment derived from Andean orogenic sources versus Amazonian cratonic sources.

**Predictions:**
- Andean-sourced sediment: higher K/Ti (less weathered), intermediate Zr
- Cratonic-sourced sediment: lower K/Ti (intensely weathered laterites), high Fe
- Provenance shifts should correlate with drainage reorganization events

**Literature Support:**
- Horton et al. (2015) documented mixed Andean-cratonic provenance in Solimoes Formation
- 87Sr/86Sr ratios in shells indicate dominant Andean drainage (Vonhof et al., 1998)
- Sedimentological evidence supports Andes as primary sediment source (Wesselingh et al., 2002)

**Limitation:** CIA-based weathering indices unreliable due to Mo tube Al detection limits and tropical weathering ceiling (CIA 80-100 throughout).

## 3. Methodological Framework

### 3.1 XRF Core Scanning Approach

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Instrument | Itrax XRF Core Scanner |
| Tube | Molybdenum (Mo) |
| Resolution | 3 mm step size |
| Exposure | Standard settings |
| Elements analyzed | Al, Si, K, Ca, Ti, Fe, Mn, Rb, Sr, Zr, Ba + trace |

### 3.2 Recommended Proxy Suite

Based on redundancy analysis and detection quality:

**Primary (Main Figures):**
- Ca - Authigenic/biogenic carbonate
- Ti - Terrigenous flux
- Fe/Mn - Redox conditions
- Zr/Rb - Grain size / hydrodynamic energy

**Secondary (Supporting):**
- Sr - Carbonate mineralogy / salinity indicator
- K/Ti - Relative weathering (with caveats)
- Rb/Sr - Inverse carbonate influence

**Contextual:**
- Inc/Coh - Matrix composition / organics
- Ba - Potential salinity indicator (not productivity)

### 3.3 Quality Control

- MSE threshold: <3.0
- CPS minimum: >20,000 cps
- Surface distance: <10 mm
- Foam exclusion zones manually defined from optical images
- Smoothing: 5-point rolling median for noise reduction

## 4. Expected Results and Significance

### 4.1 Anticipated Patterns

1. **Cyclicity:** 3-7 m parasequences should produce 5-15 geochemical cycles per section, testable against orbital periodicities
2. **Facies Association:** Geochemical proxies should discriminate lignite caps, shell beds, blue clays, and sandy intervals
3. **Site Differences:** TAM and SC geochemical fingerprints should differ systematically, supporting sub-environment interpretation
4. **Redox Zonation:** Fe/Mn should identify stratification events and help explain endemic fauna distributions

### 4.2 Significance

**Regional:** First high-resolution XRF dataset from Pebas Formation provides:
- Template for geochemical correlation across Amazonian exposures
- Independent paleoenvironmental proxy record to complement isotope studies
- Framework for identifying optimal sampling intervals for future isotope/biomarker work

**Global:** Pebas Formation represents end-member of long-lived tropical lacustrine system:
- Comparison with African Rift lakes, Eocene Green River Formation
- Test case for tropical weathering regime geochemistry
- Archive of Miocene tropical hydroclimate variability

### 5.1 Comparison with Published Pebas Paleoenvironments

| Proxy | Previous Interpretation | XRF Contribution |
|-------|------------------------|------------------|
| Mollusc assemblages | Brackish-freshwater cycles | Fe/Mn redox context |
| Ostracod ecology | Salinity/temperature | Sr/Ca carbonate type |
| 87Sr/86Sr | Water source (Andes vs. marine) | Element-level provenance support |
| δ18O/δ13C | Evaporation, productivity | Authigenic vs. detrital Ca |
| Ichnofacies | Marine influence | Independent facies characterization |

### 5.2 Paleoclimate Implications

The Middle Miocene Climatic Optimum (MMCO, 17-14 Ma) and subsequent cooling represent critical intervals:

- ITCZ migration patterns (Kaandorp et al., 2005)
- Monsoon intensification (Hoorn et al., 2022)
- Hydroclimate variability recorded in speleothems (Cheng et al., 2013)

**Key Question:** Do XRF proxies record the MMCO-to-cooling transition (~14 Ma)?

### 5.3 Biogeographic Context

The Pebas Mega-Wetland was a:
- **Barrier** to terrestrial biota exchange
- **Corridor** for marine-freshwater transitions
- **Stage** for endemic radiations (molluscs, ostracods, fishes)

**Geochemical Contribution:** Characterizing sub-environments helps explain why certain taxa occur where they do.

## 8. Results Summary

### 8.1 Dataset

| Site | Measurements | Sections | Composite Depth |
|------|--------------|----------|-----------------|
| TAM (Tamshiyacu) | 876 | 11 | 3.8 m |
| SC (Santa Corina) | 1,062 | 16 | 4.0 m |
| **Total** | **1,938** | **27** | **7.8 m** |

### 8.2 Statistical Approach

Because adjacent XRF measurements share depositional history and are autocorrelated (r ≈ 0.7), we analyzed data at the section level (n = 11 TAM, n = 16 SC) rather than treating individual measurements as independent samples. This avoids artificially inflated significance.

### 8.3 Main Finding: TAM is More Reducing Than SC

TAM sediments consistently show higher Fe/Mn ratios than SC:
- **TAM median Fe/Mn: 82** vs **SC median: 49**
- **96% of TAM** measurements exceed the reducing threshold (Fe/Mn > 50), compared to **47% at SC**
- This difference is significant (p = 0.004) and robust across all threshold values tested

**Interpretation:** TAM likely represents a more distal or restricted part of the Pebas lake system, with poorly ventilated bottom waters. SC experienced more variable redox conditions, possibly due to better water circulation or shallower depths.

### 8.4 Other Proxy Patterns (Suggestive but Not Definitive)

| Proxy | Pattern | p-value | Notes |
|-------|---------|---------|-------|
| Ti | Higher at SC | 0.01 | Suggests more terrigenous input at SC |
| Zr/Rb | Higher at TAM | 0.05 | Suggests slightly coarser grain size at TAM |
| Ca/Ti | Higher at TAM | 0.07 | Suggests more carbonate at TAM |

These patterns are suggestive but do not reach statistical significance after correcting for multiple comparisons. They warrant further investigation with larger sample sizes.

### 8.5 Spectral Analysis: No Evidence for Cyclicity

We tested for periodic variability in the longest continuous section (TAM-3A-4-5CDE-B, 191 measurements). Although apparent peaks appear at wavelengths of ~40 cm and ~95 cm, none exceed the 95% confidence threshold against red noise.

**What this means:** Autocorrelated sedimentary records commonly produce spectral peaks that look periodic but are actually noise. Our data cannot distinguish real cycles from background variability.

**What would be needed:** Radiometric dates or biostratigraphic constraints to convert depth to time, plus longer continuous records. Without age control, we cannot interpret these wavelengths in terms of orbital forcing.

### 8.6 Proxy Relationships

**Ca and Ti are nearly independent** (r = 0.06), meaning carbonate and terrigenous inputs can be tracked separately. Ca/Ti is highly correlated with raw Ca (r = 0.89), so we use raw Ca as the primary carbonate indicator.

**Zr/Rb tracks grain size**, with slightly higher values at TAM suggesting coarser sediment, though this difference is not statistically robust (p = 0.05).

### 8.7 Marine Incursions: Not Detected

We found no geochemical evidence for marine or brackish incursions. Detecting such events would require Sr isotope ratios (⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr) on shell material, which are beyond the scope of XRF scanning.

### 8.8 Depositional Environment Summary

Both sites record fine-grained, low-energy lacustrine deposition consistent with the Pebas mega-wetland model:

| Feature | TAM | SC |
|---------|-----|-----|
| Dominant redox | Strongly reducing | Variable |
| Carbonate content | Higher | Lower |
| Terrigenous input | Lower | Higher |
| Shell-rich intervals | 15% | 4% |

TAM appears to represent a more distal or restricted setting with persistent oxygen depletion, while SC shows more dynamic conditions suggesting better water circulation or proximity to sediment sources.

## 9. Summary

### What We Can Confidently Say
- TAM sediments are more reducing than SC (Fe/Mn median 82 vs 49, p = 0.004)
- This difference holds regardless of threshold choice (tested 30–100)
- Both sites show fine-grained, low-energy deposition typical of a lacustrine system
- The redox contrast suggests TAM occupied a more restricted or deeper sub-environment

### What Remains Uncertain
- Whether Ti, Zr/Rb, and Ca/Ti differences are real (suggestive patterns but not statistically robust)
- Whether apparent periodicities reflect orbital forcing (cannot distinguish from noise without age control)
- The exact mechanism behind the redox difference (water depth? organic loading? restriction?)

### What We Cannot Support
- Claims of marine incursions based on XRF data alone
- Cyclostratigraphic interpretation without radiometric or biostratigraphic age constraints
