\section{Environmental Signal Analysis}
\label{sec:environmental_analysis}

This section presents a comprehensive analysis of environmental signals preserved in the XRF geochemical record, identifying the most reliable proxies and significant stratigraphic transitions for paleoenvironmental reconstruction.

\subsection{Site-Level Environmental Differences}

Analysis of 1,979 valid XRF measurements across 27 core sections reveals highly significant geochemical differences between the Tamshiyacu (TAM; $n = 930$) and Santa Corina (SC; $n = 1,049$) localities (Figure~\ref{fig:site_comparison}).

\midrule

The data indicate that TAM represents a more \textbf{reducing, carbonate-influenced} depositional environment, while SC shows evidence of \textbf{higher oxidation and detrital input}. The 3.5-fold difference in Mn concentrations between sites is particularly diagnostic, as Mn accumulation is strongly controlled by bottom-water oxygenation.

\subsection{Proxy Reliability Ranking}

Not all geochemical proxies provide equally reliable paleoenvironmental information. We evaluated proxy reliability using three criteria: (1) coefficient of variation (CV; lower indicates more stable measurements), (2) effect size between sites (Cohen's $d$-equivalent), and (3) statistical significance of site differences (Figure~\ref{fig:proxy_reliability}).

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figures/environmental_analysis/fig2_proxy_reliability.pdf}
\caption{Proxy discriminatory power between TAM and SC sites. Effect size represents the absolute difference in medians normalized by pooled standard deviation. Proxies above the dashed line (effect size $>0.5$) show medium to large effects. Individual elements (green) generally show higher discriminatory power than ratios (purple), with Ti and Mn being the most diagnostic.}
\label{fig:proxy_reliability}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Tier 1: Highest Reliability Proxies}

\begin{description}
\item[Ti (Titanium):] CV = 0.36, effect size = 1.20. Excellent detrital proxy with very low variability and strong site discrimination. Ti is immobile during diagenesis and represents terrigenous siliciclastic input.

\item[Mn (Manganese):] CV = 0.90, effect size = 1.26. Best redox indicator despite higher variability. Mn is highly sensitive to bottom-water oxygenation, with precipitation enhanced under oxic conditions.

\item[Rb (Rubidium):] CV = 0.29, effect size = 0.88. Clay mineral proxy with excellent stability. Rb substitutes for K in illite and correlates with fine-grained sediment input.

\item[K (Potassium):] CV = 0.37, effect size = 0.76. Weathering indicator tracking clay mineral content and chemical weathering intensity in the source area.
\end{description}

\subsubsection{Tier 2: Good Reliability Proxies}

\begin{description}
\item[Fe/Mn ratio:] CV = 1.21, effect size = 0.40. Classic redox proxy despite higher variability. Values $>70$ indicate reducing conditions; TAM consistently exceeds this threshold.

\item[Mn/Ti ratio:] CV = 0.89, effect size = 0.65. Ti-normalized Mn provides an oxygenation proxy corrected for detrital dilution. Higher values indicate better bottom-water ventilation.

\item[Si/Ti ratio:] CV = 0.45, effect size = 0.82. Potential biogenic silica indicator when normalized to detrital Ti.
\end{description}

\subsubsection{Tier 3: Use with Caution}

\begin{description}
\item[Ca/Ti, Zr/Rb:] Moderate reliability with higher variability.
\item[Sr/Ca, Ca alone:] Poor site discrimination ($p > 0.4$); not recommended for site comparison.
\end{description}

\subsection{Stratigraphic Transitions and Multi-Proxy Concordance}

Major environmental transitions were identified using a rolling-window analysis (5-point window) to detect shifts $>50\%$ in key proxy values. In the stratigraphic profiles, depth increases downward following standard convention; shallower depths represent younger sediments and greater depths represent older sediments. The strongest paleoenvironmental interpretations arise when multiple independent proxies change in phase, providing concordant lines of evidence for environmental shifts. Three exemplary sections illustrate different transition styles (Figure~\ref{fig:stratigraphic_profiles}).

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/environmental_analysis/fig3_stratigraphic_profiles.pdf}

Each column shows Fe/Mn (top), Mn/Ti (middle), and Ca/Ti (bottom) for one section. Dashed horizontal line on Fe/Mn panels indicates the reducing threshold ($\sim$70). Note the different scales reflecting distinct environmental conditions at each locality.}

\subsubsection{Multi-Proxy Phase Relationships}

Within-section correlation analysis reveals systematic phase relationships among proxies that strengthen paleoenvironmental interpretations:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Fe/Mn and Mn/Ti show strong inverse correlation} ($r = -0.54$ to $-0.77$), as expected since both track redox conditions through different mechanisms: Fe/Mn increases under reducing conditions (Fe retention, Mn loss), while Mn/Ti increases under oxidizing conditions (Mn precipitation). This inverse relationship validates both proxies as independent redox indicators.

\item \textbf{Ca/Ti correlates positively with Mn/Ti} ($r = +0.24$ to $+0.45$) in TAM sections, suggesting that carbonate enrichment events often coincide with improved oxygenation---possibly reflecting periods of enhanced biological productivity and/or reduced organic matter loading.

\item \textbf{Ca/Ti correlates inversely with Fe/Mn} ($r = -0.20$ to $-0.53$), indicating that carbonate-rich intervals tend to be less reducing, consistent with shell-rich facies accumulating under better-ventilated conditions.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{TAM-1-2-3B-A (123--660 mm)}

This section records the most extreme redox variability in the dataset, with strong multi-proxy concordance ($r_{\text{Fe/Mn vs Mn/Ti}} = -0.54$; $r_{\text{Ca/Ti vs Mn/Ti}} = +0.45$):

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{129--135 mm depth:} Concordant transition where Ca/Ti increases sharply while Mn/Ti rises and Fe/Mn falls, recording a \textbf{coupled carbonate--oxygenation event} with three proxies changing in phase.

\item \textbf{177--183 mm depth:} Ca/Ti increases by 164\% coincident with Fe/Mn increase (49\%) and Mn/Ti decrease (36\%), indicating a \textbf{carbonate pulse under reducing conditions}---a distinctly different environmental mode.

\item \textbf{327--339 mm depth:} Mn/Ti increases by 60--85\% while Fe/Mn decreases by 52\%, with Ca/Ti relatively stable. This \textbf{pure redox transition} (two proxies in phase, carbonate unchanged) suggests ventilation without productivity change.

\item Fe/Mn ranges from 89 to 1327 across the full section, spanning nearly two orders of magnitude and demonstrating the section's exceptional sensitivity to redox fluctuations.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{TAM-3A-4-5CDE-B (448--1012 mm)}

This section shows the strongest redox proxy coupling ($r_{\text{Fe/Mn vs Mn/Ti}} = -0.75$) and well-defined carbonate pulses with multi-proxy concordance:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{472--478 mm depth:} \textbf{Three-proxy concordant event}---Ca/Ti increases dramatically (191--218\%), Mn/Ti increases (68--182\%), and Fe/Mn decreases (9--44\%). This represents the clearest example of a \textbf{major carbonate--oxygenation event} with all three proxies changing in expected phase relationships.

\item \textbf{457--463 mm depth:} Inverse pattern where Ca/Ti decreases (43--44\%) while Fe/Mn increases (68--82\%) and Mn/Ti decreases (37--52\%), recording a \textbf{return to reducing, carbonate-poor conditions}.

\item The strong Fe/Mn--Mn/Ti coupling ($r = -0.75$) throughout this section indicates consistent redox-driven geochemistry, making it ideal for reconstructing oxygenation history.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{SC-5-6-7ABC-B (284--692 mm)}

The best SC section shows the strongest redox proxy concordance ($r_{\text{Fe/Mn vs Mn/Ti}} = -0.77$) and multiple coupled transitions:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{290--298 mm depth:} \textbf{Sustained three-proxy concordant interval}---across 8~mm of section, Ca/Ti increases (64--156\%), Mn/Ti increases (58--104\%), and Fe/Mn decreases (35--48\%) in consistent phase. This represents a \textbf{major environmental transition} to more oxic, carbonate-rich conditions sustained over multiple measurement points.

\item Unlike TAM sections, SC shows weaker Ca/Ti correlation with redox proxies ($r \approx 0$), suggesting that carbonate and redox cycles may be partially decoupled at this site---consistent with a more dynamic, fluvially-influenced setting where detrital carbonate input may vary independently of bottom-water oxygenation.

\item The exceptional Fe/Mn--Mn/Ti coupling ($r = -0.77$) confirms robust redox signal preservation despite the more variable depositional environment.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Implications for Paleoenvironmental Reconstruction}

The multi-proxy concordance analysis identifies three distinct environmental modes recorded in the Pebas Formation:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Carbonate--oxygenation events:} Ca/Ti$\uparrow$, Mn/Ti$\uparrow$, Fe/Mn$\downarrow$ changing in phase. These likely represent periods of enhanced productivity and/or improved ventilation, with shell accumulation under oxic bottom waters.

\item \textbf{Pure redox transitions:} Mn/Ti and Fe/Mn change inversely while Ca/Ti remains stable. These reflect oxygenation changes independent of carbonate production, possibly driven by water column mixing or circulation changes.

\item \textbf{Reducing carbonate events:} Ca/Ti increases while Fe/Mn increases and Mn/Ti decreases. These record carbonate accumulation under oxygen-depleted conditions, possibly from authigenic carbonate precipitation or shell transport from shallower, oxic waters.
\end{enumerate}

The strongest paleoenvironmental interpretations derive from intervals where $\geq$3 proxies change concordantly, as single-proxy excursions may reflect local diagenesis or analytical artifacts. We identify 15 such multi-proxy concordant intervals across the three key sections, providing robust anchor points for environmental reconstruction.

The relationship between Fe/Mn and Mn/Ti provides a two-dimensional framework for interpreting redox conditions (Figure~\ref{fig:redox_crossplot}).

\subsubsection{Tamshiyacu (TAM) Environment}

TAM sediments consistently plot in the \textbf{reducing field} (high Fe/Mn, low Mn/Ti), indicating:
\begin{itemize}
\item Oxygen-depleted bottom waters
\item Possible water column stratification
\item Limited ventilation or mixing
\item More distal or restricted lake position
\end{itemize}

The median Fe/Mn of 95.1 exceeds the reducing threshold (70) in 96\% of measurements, indicating persistent anoxic or dysoxic conditions.

\subsubsection{Santa Corina (SC) Environment}

SC sediments show more variable redox conditions with a shift toward the \textbf{oxidizing field}:
\begin{itemize}
\item Higher bottom-water oxygen levels
\item Enhanced Mn precipitation ($3.5\times$ higher than TAM)
\item Better-ventilated conditions
\item Proximity to oxygenated inflow or shallower water depth
\end{itemize}

The median Fe/Mn of 50.8 and Mn/Ti of 0.36 suggest periodic ventilation events punctuating generally reducing conditions.

\subsection{Environmental Synthesis}

The geochemical data support a model where TAM and SC represent distinct depositional environments within the Pebas mega-wetland system:

\begin{description}
\item[TAM:] More \textbf{restricted lacustrine setting} with persistent water column stratification, reduced bottom-water oxygen, elevated carbonate production or preservation, and lower siliciclastic dilution. This environment may represent a more distal position within the lake system or a protected embayment with limited circulation.

\item[SC:] More \textbf{dynamic fluvially-influenced setting} with better bottom-water ventilation, higher detrital input (50\% more Ti), enhanced Mn cycling, and more variable environmental conditions. This environment suggests proximity to fluvial sources or a more open-water position subject to periodic mixing.
\end{description}

The preserved geochemical variability within sections (particularly TAM-1-2-3B-A) indicates that environmental conditions fluctuated substantially through time, as recorded in the stratigraphic succession from older (deeper) to younger (shallower) sediments. Individual core sections span timescales estimated at $10^2$--$10^4$ years based on typical Pebas Formation sedimentation rates. These temporal fluctuations likely reflect climate-driven changes in lake level, productivity, and/or runoff intensity.

\subsection{Recommendations for Future Work}

Based on this analysis, we recommend:

\begin{enumerate}
\item Use \textbf{Fe/Mn} as the primary redox proxy (highest significance)
\item Normalize elements to \textbf{Ti} for detrital correction (most reliable element)
\item Focus detailed paleoenvironmental reconstruction on \textbf{TAM-1-2-3B-A} (extreme variability) and \textbf{TAM-3A-4-5CDE-B} (clear events)
\item Use \textbf{SC-5-6-7ABC-B} as the representative SC comparison section
\item Avoid sections with $<30$ measurements or $<100$ mm stratigraphic coverage
\item Consider paired Fe/Mn--Mn/Ti analysis for comprehensive redox interpretation
\end{enumerate}
