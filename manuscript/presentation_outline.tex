\section{Introduction: Ti-Normalized Proxy Ratios}
%==============================================================================

Titanium (Ti) serves as an ideal normalizing element for XRF-based paleoenvironmental reconstruction because it is:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Terrigenous in origin (derived from detrital silicates)
    \item Chemically immobile under diagenetic conditions
    \item Insensitive to redox changes
\end{itemize}

Normalizing Ca, Fe, and Mn to Ti isolates the \textbf{authigenic} (in-situ formed) component of these elements from the detrital background, revealing paleoenvironmental signals.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Ti-normalized proxy interpretation}
\begin{tabular}{llll}
\toprule
\textbf{Ratio} & \textbf{Primary Signal} & \textbf{High Values} & \textbf{Low Values} \\
\midrule
\textcolor{CaTi}{\textbf{Ca/Ti}} & Carbonate input & Shell accumulation, alkaline & Terrigenous-dominated \\
\textcolor{FeTi}{\textbf{Fe/Ti}} & Iron enrichment & Reducing conditions (Fe mobile) & Oxic (Fe locked in oxides) \\
\textcolor{MnTi}{\textbf{Mn/Ti}} & Manganese enrichment & Oxic (Mn precipitates as MnO$_2$) & Reducing (Mn mobile) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=blue!5, colframe=blue!50!black, title=Key Principle]
\textbf{Fe and Mn have opposite redox behavior:}
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
    \item Under \textbf{reducing} conditions: Fe mobilizes (high Fe/Ti), Mn escapes (low Mn/Ti)
    \item Under \textbf{oxidizing} conditions: Fe locked in oxides (low Fe/Ti), Mn precipitates (high Mn/Ti)
\end{itemize}
Therefore, the \textbf{Fe/Ti : Mn/Ti ratio} is a powerful redox discriminator.
\end{tcolorbox}

\textbf{Key observations from TAM core top:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textcolor{CaTi}{\textbf{Ca/Ti is elevated}} (median 2.42): Strong carbonate signal, visible shell-rich horizons
    \item \textcolor{FeTi}{\textbf{Fe/Ti is moderate-high}} (median 15.7): Iron enrichment indicates reducing conditions
    \item \textcolor{MnTi}{\textbf{Mn/Ti is low}} (median 0.21): Manganese depletion confirms bottom-water dysoxia
\end{itemize}

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=orange!5, colframe=orange!50!black, title=TAM Interpretation]
The combination of \textbf{high Ca/Ti}, \textbf{high Fe/Ti}, and \textbf{low Mn/Ti} indicates a \textbf{stratified, dysoxic lacustrine environment} with:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
    \item Carbonate-supersaturated surface waters supporting mollusc communities
    \item Oxygen-depleted bottom waters where Fe remains mobile and Mn escapes to the water column
    \item \textbf{Excellent preservation potential} for articulated fossils
\end{itemize}
\end{tcolorbox}

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=blue!5, colframe=blue!50!black, title=SC Interpretation]
The combination of \textbf{lower Ca/Ti}, \textbf{high Fe/Ti}, and \textbf{higher Mn/Ti} indicates a \textbf{dynamic, ventilated fluvio-lacustrine environment} with:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
    \item Lower carbonate production or more terrigenous dilution
    \item Periodically oxygenated bottom waters allowing Mn precipitation
    \item Iron enrichment from reducing sediment porewaters
\end{itemize}
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection{Cross-Proxy Relationships}

\begin{figure}[H]

    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../output/figures/cross_proxy/crossplots.png}

    \label{fig:crossplots}

\textbf{Crossplot interpretations:}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Mn/Ti vs Fe/Ti (Left)}: Both sites show negative correlation (expected: reducing conditions deplete Mn while enriching Fe). TAM occupies the low-Mn/high-Fe space (more reducing).

    \item \textbf{Ca/Ti vs Mn/Ti (Center)}: TAM shows high Ca/Ti at low Mn/Ti (carbonate-rich dysoxic). SC shows lower Ca/Ti across a range of Mn/Ti (carbonate-poor, variable redox).

    \item \textbf{Fe/Ti vs Ca/Ti (Right)}: No strong correlation at either site, suggesting Fe and Ca are independently controlled (Fe by redox, Ca by biogenic input).
\end{enumerate}

%==============================================================================

The 171 kyr overlap period provides a critical test: if site differences are due to \textbf{spatial heterogeneity}, they should persist during time-equivalent intervals. If due to \textbf{temporal evolution}, they should disappear.

\subsection{Conceptual Model}

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=green!5, colframe=green!50!black, title=Pebas Mega-Wetland: Spatial Heterogeneity]
Within the $>$1 million km$^2$ Pebas mega-wetland, \textbf{two distinct environmental modes coexisted}:

\textbf{\textcolor{TAMcolor}{TAM-type (Distal Lacustrine)}}:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
    \item Deep, stratified lake center
    \item Persistent bottom-water dysoxia (low O$_2$)
    \item High carbonate productivity (mollusc-rich)
    \item \textbf{Taphonomic sweet spot}: excellent fossil preservation
\end{itemize}

\textbf{\textcolor{SCcolor}{SC-type (Fluvio-Lacustrine)}}:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
    \item Shallower, river-influenced margins
    \item Periodically ventilated (seasonal mixing, floods)
    \item More terrigenous input, less carbonate
    \item \textbf{Dynamic environment}: variable preservation
\end{itemize}

These sites were separated by only $\sim$30 km, demonstrating \textbf{environmental heterogeneity} within the mega-wetland.
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection{TAM as Taphonomic Sweet Spot}

The TAM core top signature (\textbf{high Ca/Ti + low Mn/Ti}) represents the optimal conditions for exceptional preservation:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Low oxygen inhibits scavenging and bioturbation
    \item Alkaline, carbonate-rich waters prevent shell dissolution
    \item Rapid burial in organic-rich sediments
\end{itemize}

This explains the remarkable fossil diversity observed in TAM-type deposits, including articulated crocodilians, complete mollusc assemblages, and preserved soft tissue traces.

\section{Conclusions}
%==============================================================================

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Cross-proxy analysis} using Ca/Ti, Fe/Ti, and Mn/Ti effectively discriminates paleoenvironmental conditions in the Pebas Formation.

    \item \textbf{TAM core top} ($\sim$12.9 Ma) shows high Ca/Ti (3.1$\times$ SC), and low Mn/Ti (0.69$\times$ SC), indicating a \textbf{carbonate-rich, dysoxic} environment.

    \item \textbf{Overlap period analysis} ($\sim$13.4 Ma) reveals that TAM was \textbf{even more reducing} (Mn/Ti only 0.32$\times$ SC) during time-equivalent deposition.

    \item The \textbf{site contrast strengthens} during the overlap, with TAM Mn/Ti dropping from 0.21 (top) to 0.10 (overlap), confirming \textbf{persistent spatial heterogeneity}.

    \item \textbf{SC remained stable} (Mn/Ti $\approx$ 0.31) throughout, indicating consistent environmental conditions at the fluvio-lacustrine site.

    \item The TAM proxy signature (\textbf{high Ca/Ti + low Mn/Ti}) defines the \textbf{taphonomic sweet spot} for exceptional fossil preservation in the Pebas mega-wetland.
\end{enumerate}
